# Control Framed as Protection  
## An Evidence-Led Investigation into the UK’s Online Safety Regime

**By UKIT**  
**Date: 14 April 2026 (Europe/London)**

---

## Executive Summary

The Online Safety Act 2023 is repeatedly described as a child-protection and harm-reduction project. That claim is not empty. Online harms are real, severe, and measurable. The Act frames its purpose as making internet services safer, using a *safe by design* model while explicitly committing to freedom of expression and privacy.

This investigation argues that the Act should be **repealed and replaced**.

In practice, its structure and incentives frequently function as **control framed as protection**, encouraging:

- Pre-emptive suppression of speech  
- Identity and age-gating of general access  
- Expansion of surveillance-adjacent systems  

These outcomes are not driven by a single clause, but by the **combined regulatory architecture**:

- Broad and expandable harm definitions  
- Mandatory risk-based system design  
- Severe enforcement penalties  

The result is predictable: platforms rationally **over-comply**.

Government impact assessments acknowledge:

- High compliance costs  
- Unmonetised impacts on privacy and expression  
- Limited empirical certainty  

This report treats “corruption shown as protection” not as misconduct, but as a **structural risk pattern** in regulatory design.

---

## Background and Legal Structure

The regime applies across the UK under the Online Safety Act 2023.

Core features:

- Duties to manage **illegal content**
- Duties to protect **children from harmful content**
- Enforcement by **Ofcom**

The Act introduces:

- A *safe by design* requirement  
- System-level obligations (not just content removal)  
- Categorisation of services (Category 1, 2A, 2B)

## The “Control Surface” of the Act

### 1. Safe by Design → System Control

Safety becomes a **design requirement**, not just moderation.

Implication:
- Platforms must redesign feeds, access, and interaction systems  
- Compliance affects **how content is distributed**, not just removed  

---

### 2. Expanding Harm Definitions

- Illegal content duties include **risk-based prevention**
- Priority offences can be expanded over time

Example:
- Cyberflashing and self-harm encouragement elevated in 2025

Effect:
- The “harm perimeter” is **not fixed**

---

### 3. Children’s Safety Duties

- Prevent exposure to “harmful content”
- Require **age verification/estimation**

Effect:
- Creates a **legal equivalent of “lawful but harmful” control** for minors  
- Expands enforcement into general platform access  

---

### 4. Age Assurance → Identity Infrastructure

“Highly effective” age checks include:

- Photo ID verification  
- Facial age estimation  
- Open banking checks  
- Digital identity systems  

Implications:

- Routine processing of sensitive personal data  
- Erosion of anonymity  
- Potential centralised identity layers  

---

### 5. Enforcement Pressure

Maximum penalties:

- £18 million or  
- 10% of global revenue  

Additional powers:

- Access restriction orders  
- Business disruption orders  

Effect:

- Over-compliance becomes rational  
- Smaller platforms disproportionately affected

## Economic and Structural Impact

Government modelling identifies:

- High compliance costs:
  - Moderation systems  
  - Age verification infrastructure  
  - Regulatory fees  

Limitations:

- Freedom of expression impacts **not monetised**  
- Privacy impacts **qualitative only**  

Expected outcomes:

- Market consolidation  
- Reduced innovation  
- Exit of smaller services  

---

## Privacy Trade-Offs

Key tension:

> Protect children → collect more data  
> Protect privacy → collect less data  

Reality:

- Age assurance requires personal data processing  
- Often includes biometric systems  
- Enforcement pressures favour **data expansion over minimisation**

## Regulatory Capture Risk Indicators

This report identifies structural indicators:

### 1. Ministerial Influence
- Power to guide and modify regulatory codes  
- Strategic priorities influence enforcement direction  

### 2. Industry-Funded Regulation
- Ofcom funded via regulated companies  
- Potential dependency on large firms  

### 3. Safety-Tech Market Expansion
- Growth incentives for compliance technologies  
- Potential vendor influence

## Counterarguments and Rebuttals

### Claim: Strong regulation is necessary

**Response:**  
Necessity does not justify **any** regulatory model. Proportionality remains key.

---

### Claim: Safeguards exist

**Response:**  
Incentives override safeguards.  
Penalty structures drive conservative enforcement.

---

### Claim: Age assurance can be privacy-friendly

**Response:**  
Even minimal systems require identity inference.  
Scaling creates systemic impact.

---

## Policy Recommendation: Repeal and Replace

### 1. Narrow the Scope

- Focus on **clearly illegal content**
- Remove broad “duty of care” model

---

### 2. Require Due Process

- Judicial oversight for:
  - Blocking  
  - Monitoring tools  
- Transparent enforcement thresholds  

---

### 3. Restrict Age Assurance

- Limit to **high-risk contexts only**
- Enforce:
  - Data minimisation  
  - No biometric retention  
- Promote tokenised verification (e.g. “over 18” proof only)

---

### 4. Protect Encryption

- Prohibit systemic scanning of private messages  
- Use targeted, warrant-based investigations  

---

### 5. Reduce Political Influence

- Limit ministerial direction powers  
- Increase transparency and parliamentary oversight  

---

### 6. Introduce Evidence-Based Review

- Independent evaluation metrics  
- Sunset clauses for intrusive measures  

---

## Conclusion

The Online Safety Act 2023 is not simply a safety framework.

It represents a **fundamental shift in internet governance**, moving toward:

- Continuous platform regulation  
- Identity-based access  
- Proactive system control  

Early enforcement demonstrates:

- Expansion of age verification  
- Platform restrictions and geoblocking  
- Increased data collection pressures  

The issue is not whether harm exists.

It is whether the response is **proportionate, targeted, and rights-preserving**.

This investigation finds that the current model is not.
