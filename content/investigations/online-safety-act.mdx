## Summary

The Online Safety Act is heralded by the UK government as a once‑in‑a‑generation
law to make the country “the safest place in the world to be online.” It
imposes legal duties on user‑to‑user services and search engines to tackle
illegal content, protect children and conduct risk assessments. Supporters
say that such regulation is overdue and will reduce online abuse. Critics
counter that the Act hands sweeping powers to regulators, forces
privacy‑invasive age checks and could lead to blanket surveillance. This
investigation explores the law’s provisions, the evidence for and against it,
how it is being implemented and its unfolding impacts on citizens and
platforms.

## Key Points

- **Duties and fines** – Platforms must remove illegal content, assess the
  risk of harm to children and comply with Ofcom codes of practice. Failure
  can result in fines of up to £18 million or 10 % of global turnover【915852258660772†L199-L232】.
- **Age assurance requirements** – The Act introduces mandatory age
  verification for pornography and high‑risk services. Regulators insist
  that self‑declaration is insufficient and that highly effective,
  risk‑based methods be used【668258633734839†L31-L67】.
- **Privacy and censorship concerns** – Digital rights groups warn that
  mandatory age checks and monitoring duties undermine privacy and could
  force providers to scan private messages【626991041819174†L140-L161】.
- **Real‑world consequences** – Early implementation has already led to a
  surge in VPN downloads, some services ceasing UK operations and a
  petition calling for the Act’s repeal【728410815567409†L81-L116】【658625671877330†L160-L195】.
- **Evolving amendments** – Proposals in 2026 would allow the Secretary of
  State to ban under‑18s from accessing certain online services, raising
  further questions about unchecked censorship powers【32894963678392†L146-L170】.

## What Happened

### From bill to law

The idea of an Online Safety Bill emerged in the late 2010s amid concerns
about cyberbullying, terrorist propaganda and the accessibility of
pornography to children. After years of consultation and multiple drafts,
Parliament passed the bill, and it received Royal Assent on 26 October 2023,
becoming the Online Safety Act. The statute creates new duties for
user‑to‑user services and search engines to prevent the spread of illegal
content and protect children from harmful material【57844258803045†L140-L167】.
It appoints Ofcom as the regulator with enforcement powers.

The Act’s scope is broad. Services must conduct “children’s risk
assessments,” identify categories of content that are illegal or harmful
for children, and implement measures to mitigate those risks. For high‑risk
areas, such as pornography, the law requires age verification. Ofcom can
issue fines of up to £18 million or 10 % of a company’s global turnover for
non‑compliance【915852258660772†L199-L232】.

### Age verification rules

Under the Act, pornography websites and certain social platforms must verify
that users are adults before allowing access. In guidance published in
2025, Ofcom listed examples of acceptable methods: facial age estimation,
credit‑card checks and digital identity wallets【915852258660772†L254-L266】.
The regulators have made clear that self‑declaration (“I am over 18”) is
insufficient and that highly effective, risk‑based age assurance is
required【668258633734839†L31-L67】. Companies that fail to implement these
measures can face hefty fines and even criminal liability for executives.

### Implementation and backlash

On 25 July 2025, the Act’s age‑verification requirements came into force
【915852258660772†L199-L232】. In the weeks that followed, the
Information Technology & Innovation Foundation (ITIF) reported a spike in
VPN downloads as users sought to bypass age checks【728410815567409†L81-L116】.
Some adult websites temporarily blocked UK visitors or shut down entirely,
citing the cost and legal risk of compliance. Digital rights groups
warned that mandatory age verification compromised user privacy and might
force platforms to scan private messages for prohibited content【626991041819174†L140-L161】.

By September 2025, a coalition of activists launched a petition calling for
the repeal of the Online Safety Act, arguing that its harms to privacy and
free expression outweighed any benefits【658625671877330†L160-L195】. The
campaign gathered tens of thousands of signatures but did not persuade the
government to revoke the law. Instead, regulators reiterated that
age‑assurance measures must be technology‑neutral and risk‑based【668258633734839†L31-L67】.

### New amendments

In March 2026, the House of Lords considered amendments that would allow
the Secretary of State to ban under‑18s from using certain user‑to‑user
services【32894963678392†L146-L170】. Although the Commons rejected an earlier
blanket ban, the proposed new power would enable ministers to restrict
access to specific features or platforms. Critics fear that this amounts
to open‑ended censorship; supporters argue it provides flexibility to
protect children. At the same time, the Mental Health Foundation called
for restrictions on platforms that fail to meet safety standards and
emphasised the need for offline support for young people【472249431651222†L186-L246】.

## Supporters’ View

Supporters of the Online Safety Act argue that the status quo—where
children can access pornography or be exposed to abuse—was untenable.
Official guidance emphasises that the law imposes clear duties on
providers to remove illegal content, protect children and take action
against harmful material【57844258803045†L140-L167】. The government
maintains that Ofcom’s powers are necessary to ensure compliance and that
fines are proportionate to the harm inflicted.

Child‑protection advocates believe robust age verification is essential.
They point to research suggesting that early exposure to sexual content can
harm young people and that self‑declaration fails to keep children off
pornography sites. The Mental Health Foundation has backed
restrictions on platforms that do not meet safety standards and stressed
that online safety must be complemented by offline support networks【472249431651222†L186-L246】.

Regulators also defend the law as technology‑neutral. In a joint
statement, the ICO and Ofcom said that services must adopt
risk‑proportionate and highly effective age assurance; they emphasised that
data processing must comply with existing data‑protection law【668258633734839†L31-L67】.

## Critics’ View

Digital rights organisations such as the Electronic Frontier Foundation
contend that the Online Safety Act will normalise general monitoring of
user content. They warn that mandatory age checks could require service
providers to scan private messages or create backdoors to encrypted
communications【626991041819174†L140-L161】. Because age verification often
relies on collecting sensitive personal data—credit cards, face scans or
government IDs—critics argue that the Act threatens privacy and could
lead to data breaches.

Economists and technologists note the unintended consequences. In the
weeks after age‑verification rules took effect, VPN usage rose sharply and
some sites simply withdrew from the UK market【728410815567409†L81-L116】. This
not only undermines the goal of keeping children away from harmful
content but may also push users towards services beyond UK jurisdiction.

Civil‑liberties groups fear the Act sets a precedent for broader
censorship. The petition to repeal the law stresses that the vague
definitions of “harmful” content could be abused to suppress legitimate
speech【658625671877330†L160-L195】. The proposed 2026 amendment allowing
ministers to ban under‑18s from certain services further heightens
concerns about government overreach【32894963678392†L146-L170】.

## Public Impact

Although the core provisions of the Online Safety Act are still being
implemented, early evidence suggests significant disruption. The
age‑verification mandate has already led to a spike in VPN downloads and
service withdrawals【728410815567409†L81-L116】. Some UK‑based content
creators fear their work will be misclassified as “harmful” and
down‑ranked or removed. The requirement to monitor user‑generated content
may disproportionately burden small platforms that lack resources to
comply.

There is also a risk of chilling speech. The Act’s broad
definitions of “legal but harmful” content leave room for subjective
interpretation. Critics worry that platforms will err on the side of
removal to avoid fines, leading to over‑moderation and suppression of
marginalised voices. On the other hand, supporters argue that clearer
rules will give victims of abuse better tools to demand removal of harmful
posts.

## Editorial Analysis

The Online Safety Act seeks to balance two competing imperatives: the
protection of children and users from genuinely harmful content and the
preservation of fundamental rights to privacy and free expression. In
practice, the law tilts strongly toward precaution, empowering regulators
to impose hefty fines and compelling platforms to deploy intrusive
age‑verification systems.

Evidence from the first year of implementation suggests the approach may
backfire. The surge in VPN downloads and the withdrawal of some services
show that users and companies alike will seek to circumvent or avoid
onerous compliance measures【728410815567409†L81-L116】. Meanwhile, the
petition to repeal the Act underscores the depth of public unease
【658625671877330†L160-L195】. A more effective strategy might involve
targeted education, robust data‑protection guarantees and transparency
around how harmful content is defined and enforced. Without these, the
Online Safety Act risks trading one set of harms for another.

## Owner’s Personal Opinion

<OpinionWarning>
The Online Safety Act is a case study in well‑intentioned but heavy‑handed
legislation. As an editor, I appreciate the need to protect children and
curb harassment. Yet I cannot ignore the chilling effect of giving the
state and regulators sweeping authority to monitor our private
communications and demand proof of age. Turning every internet user into
a suspect until proven adult is not a sustainable or ethical approach.

Instead of reaching for surveillance tools, lawmakers should invest in
digital literacy, empower parents and teachers and fund independent
research into harms. The current approach feels like using a hammer to
perform surgery – blunt, imprecise and liable to cause collateral damage.
If we truly care about safety, we must build systems that respect
individual rights while targeting bad actors, rather than punishing the
entire population.
</OpinionWarning>
